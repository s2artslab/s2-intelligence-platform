# Pro se litigation — procedural fundamentals

## Deadlines and calendaring

Federal and state courts treat deadline computation as mandatory, not advisory. Missing a response deadline often results in default or dismissal regardless of the merits. Always verify whether the clock runs from service, filing, or a specific event, and whether state rules modify federal timing when both apply.

## Local rules

Many districts have local rules that override or supplement general civil procedure. These may require specific cover pages, font sizes, line numbering, meet-and-confer certificates, or page limits on motions. A filing that complies with national form rules can still be rejected for local rule violations.

## Motions practice

Dispositive and non-dispositive motions typically require a notice of motion, memorandum of points and authorities, declarations or affidavits where facts are disputed, and a proposed order. A letter to the judge is not a substitute for a properly noticed motion in most courts.

## Pro se expectations

Courts may extend some leniency to pro se parties on formatting, but rarely on core deadlines or service requirements. Litigants should document service meticulously and retain proof of filing timestamps.

## Research discipline

Legal research for pro se work should prioritize controlling authority in the correct jurisdiction, then persuasive authority. Secondary sources and AI summaries require verification against primary sources before reliance in filings.
